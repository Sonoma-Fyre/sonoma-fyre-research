## Our subject
Main character: a struggling boutique Sonoma winery. Lead candidates are Monte Rio Cellars or Quivira Vineyards, to confirm through July interviews (the outline's own risk plan allows the protagonist to change if outreach stalls). Contrast cases already named in the plan: adapting Sonoma wineries (Two Shepherds, Idlewild), out-of-region backups (OneHope and Beringer in Napa, JM Cellars in Washington), and non-alcoholic brands (Saint Viviana, Tomorrow Cellars).

The winery is the character we follow; the shifting consumer is the demand it has to win back. That keeps us inside the locked customer-as-hero method: the winery is the protagonist of the case, the buyer is the hero whose changed behavior drives every act.

Note: none of the nine desk sources names Monte Rio Cellars or Quivira. Everything winery-specific (its buyers, economics, portfolio, pivots) is a primary-research gap, not a desk finding.

## The through-line
A legacy winery is caught between a traditional customer base that is aging out and drinking less, and a younger, wellness-minded buyer it has not captured, while supply-chain inflation has turned mid-tier wine into an unsustainable luxury. The sharpest single contradiction to anchor the story: the same wellness-driven consumers who are fueling a boom in non-alcoholic beer and mocktails are rejecting non-alcoholic wine (nbc-2025).

## Act I: The Diagnosis (Assets vs. Audience)
The outline's Act I question: is the winery losing its traditional loyal customers (attrition), or failing to acquire the new younger demographic (acquisition)? Desk data cannot answer that for our winery yet, but it frames the diagnosis in three layers.

**The macro decline (the weather system).**
Wine demand is in a sustained, structural fall, not a cyclical dip. Global consumption dropped to 208 million hectolitres in 2025, down 2.7% on 2024 and about 14% since 2018 (oiv-2025). The United States, still the world's largest wine market at roughly a 15.3% share, is now part of the decline: US consumption fell 4.3% to 31.9 million hectolitres, down from 33.3 million in 2024 (oiv-2025). Commercial sales data agree from another angle: US wine sales fell about 6% from 2023 to 2024 (SipSource, via nbc-2025), and the West Coast winery count dropped 4.3% in a single year, so producers are exiting, not just selling less (sonoma-mag-2025). Global production sat 9.4% below its five-year average and world vineyard area contracted for a sixth straight year (oiv-2025). One caveat to carry: OIV consumption is an apparent-consumption estimate (production plus imports minus exports) that the OIV itself says warrants careful interpretation (oiv-2025).

Added 2026-07-15, full-year 2025 and early 2026 data: the decline continued but decelerated on most measures. SVB estimates the 2025 US market at about 329 million cases (from 335.9 million in 2024) and about $74.3 billion (from $75.5 billion), so down 2.0% by volume and 1.6% by dollars (svb-2026). IWSR puts total US beverage alcohol at -5% volume in 2025, with wine -6%, beer -6%, spirits -4%, and no-alcohol beer +15% (iwsr-2026-may). Distributor depletions were steeper: wine down 9% in volume and 6% in value in 2025 (SipSource, via terrain-2026). Apparent consumption was 870 million gallons in 2024, down from 901 million in 2023 (bw166/Gomberg, via wine-institute-2026). Consumer spending stayed above $115 billion, value up 3% on falling volume, so higher prices are masking the volume slide (bmo-2026). Winery direct-to-consumer shipments fell 15% by volume in 2025, the steepest contraction in that dataset's record (sovos-2026). On drinking itself, 54% of US adults now say they drink, the lowest in Gallup's near-90-year trend, and average consumption is 2.8 drinks a week, the lowest since its 1996 tracking began (gallup-2025). Trajectory: 2026 year-to-date off-premise table wine is down 3.8% by volume versus 6% for full-year 2025 (shanken-2026); distributor forecasts stay negative through 2026 (wswa-2026); SVB expects "the market bottoming in 2027 through 2028" and warns "This is not a cycle you can wait out" (svb-2026); 71% of surveyed wineries expect stabilization or rebound within three years (bmo-2026).

**The Sonoma squeeze (where it lands).**
Locally the decline shows up as a grape glut and a visitor slump. About 30% of Sonoma County wine grapes are projected to go uncontracted in 2025, left on the vine, sold at a loss into bulk, or discounted, and grape prices are expected to fall roughly 7% (sonoma-edc-2025). This is central to the local economy: wine is nearly 30% of Sonoma's agricultural jobs and 46% of county land is farmed (sonoma-edc-2025). Manufacturing, which includes much of the county's winemaking, shed roughly 1,600 to 1,800 jobs since February 2020 (sonoma-edc-2025); it is one of the weaker sectors, though not the single largest job loser, so we should avoid calling it the hardest-hit. The demand problem is visible at the cellar door: Sonoma tasting-room visits dropped 14% in 2024 and a further 8.4% in early 2025, hitting small, family-owned wineries the hardest (press-democrat-2025), while county hotel revenue per available room ($117 as of June 2025) runs below both last year and pre-pandemic levels (sonoma-edc-2025). Established names are already retrenching: Sbragia Family Vineyards closed its Geyserville tasting room in 2024, and Healdsburg's Arista sold its winery and estate vineyard while keeping the brand (sonoma-mag-2025). US alcohol consumption sits at a 90-year low (sonoma-edc-2025).

Added 2026-07-15: the glut resolved into a historic supply correction. The 2025 California crush was "the lightest on record since 1999" at 2.62 million tons, 8% below 2024 and 23% below the five-year average, with an estimated 57,000 acres removed statewide and crop value down 22% against the five-year average; Sonoma County Chardonnay averaged $2,370 a ton on contract while spot prices ran "closer to $800 per ton" (turrentine-2026). In SVB's June 2026 survey, the average Sonoma bottle SRP is $63.66 versus Napa's $103.15, and 30% of Sonoma respondents cut tasting fees in 2025; among fee reducers nationally, about a quarter saw visitation clearly improve and another 27% saw it stabilize (napa-register-2026). Institutionally, Sonoma County Vintners and Sonoma County Winegrowers announced they will unify into a single "Sonoma County Wine" membership beginning 2027 (wia-2026).

**The buyer shift (attrition, acquisition, and substitution).**
The demand loss is less "people dislike wine" and more a reprioritization around health, moderation, and variety, and the desk data shows both halves of the winery's problem.
- Acquisition gap (the young are not entering wine): among 18-to-34-year-olds the share who drink at all fell from 72% to 62% over two decades, and their average drinks per week fell from 5.2 to 3.6 (gallup-2023-saad). Crucially, when young adults do drink, they choose beer or liquor over wine, while wine remains the drink of choice of older, more affluent, more female, college-educated buyers (gallup-2024-brenan). Gallup names the young cohort's growing racial and ethnic diversity, not health alone, as the main reason for its decline (gallup-2023-saad). Added 2026-07-15: Gallup's 2025 poll shows 18-to-34 participation fell from 59% in 2023 to 50% in 2025 (gallup-2025), a sharp post-2023 acceleration on top of the two-decade drift (see contradiction 2, now resolved). Among 21-to-39 drinkers who are reducing wine, 47% cite rising prices of the wines they like, and wine trails beer on value perception, 50% versus 57% rating it high value (wine-opinions-2025).
- Health and pharmacological headwind (hits all ages): a record 45% of US adults now say moderate drinking is bad for health, up 17 points since 2018 and reaching 65% among 18-to-34-year-olds (gallup-2024-brenan). GLP-1 drugs deepen it: 44% of users drink less and 82% keep drinking less after stopping, and wine is the steepest cutback of any category (52%, versus 43% for beer and 40% for spirits) (ey-parthenon-2025). Added 2026-07-15: by 2025 a majority of all adults, 53%, call moderate drinking bad for health, 66% among 18-to-34s (gallup-2025). EY's November 2025 update of the same page reaffirms the 44% and 82% GLP-1 figures and projects low- and no-alcohol at a 13.6% CAGR (2024 to 2029) versus 2.1% for traditional alcohol (ey-2025-glp1-update).
- Substitution and affordability: buyers trade toward convenience and lower intensity. Ready-to-drink premixed cocktails are one of the few growth areas, cannabis is "taking a big chunk out of it," and non-alcoholic beer and spirits nearly tripled in sales since 2019 (nbc-2025), with low- and no-alcohol projected to grow 13.6% a year through 2029 against 2.1% for traditional alcohol (ey-parthenon-2025). Wine also carries a convenience penalty (bottle, corkscrew, glasses) against premixed formats (nbc-2025). On price, wine has grown expensive relative to rivals ($10 to $14 per litre, up about 40% since 2000) (nbc-2025), and OIV notes global export prices remain near record highs even as volumes fall, so high prices "continued to weigh on demand" (oiv-2025). Added 2026-07-15: the non-alcoholic split is now quantified: US NA off-premise sales are $823 million (up 27.2%), of which NA beer holds 84.9% and NA wine 11.2%, and NA overall is 0.7% of alcohol sales (anba-2025). Cannabis substitution has direct evidence among users: in a University at Buffalo study of 438 past-year cannabis users, cannabis-beverage consumers cut weekly drinks from 7.02 to 3.35 and 62.6% reduced or stopped drinking, though it is a convenience sample, not population-level attribution (sciencedaily-2026).
- Wine-buyer tradeoffs, now measured (added 2026-07-15; this was our biggest stated gap): in a January 2026 Wine Opinions survey of 1,351 US wine drinkers, a net 16% drink wine less often than one to two years ago; among reducers, 49% cite cutting back on beverage alcohol in general, 38% health and wellness, and 24% that preferred wines became too expensive. Price dominates the spend decision instead: 51% of reducers agree "I can't afford to spend as much on wine as I have in the past," 47% found cheaper alternatives, and 54% of all respondents buy under-$15 wine weekly or monthly versus 28% for $30-plus (drinks-business-2026). IWSR now names cost the most common US moderation driver, cited by 31% of drinkers (iwsr-2026-apr). Cohort structure shifted: millennials are now the largest wine-drinking cohort at 31% (boomers 26%, down from 32% in 2023; Gen Z 14%, up from 9%), while adults drinking wine at least every two to three months fell from 34% to 29%, about 76 million consumers, roughly 9 million fewer than 2023; among millennials who do not drink wine, 35% dislike the taste and 39% prefer the taste of other drinks, so preference, not only price or health, blocks adoption (wmc-2025).
- Price sensitivity, now quantified (added 2026-07-15; the gaps list said no estimate existed): aggregate US wine demand is inelastic, about -0.53 on 2007-2019 scanner data, while demand facing an individual wine product averages about -4.77 (chandra-2025). The meta-analytic literature agrees on the category: mean -0.65, median -0.55, with 83% of estimates inelastic (fogarty-2008). Implication: category-wide price cuts cannot restore demand, but a single winery's repricing can move share powerfully. Tier data locates the pressure: under-$12 is deteriorating fastest while $20-to-$29 and $100-plus show growth expectations, the "hourglass" (svb-2026); the fastest-growing brands are priced well above the $8.64 table-wine retail average (shanken-2026); super-premium volume rose 1% against the negative trend (iwsr-2026-apr).

Diagnosis takeaway (framed, not concluded): the desk data shows both an acquisition gap and a broad health-and-affordability headwind, but cannot tell us which dominates for our winery's own buyers. The 2026-07-15 buyer data sharpens the split: stated reasons for drinking wine less often lean moderation and health, stated reasons for spending less lean price. Settling which binds for our winery's buyers is the job of Act II.

## Act III: The Pivot Execution
Still needs primary research for our own winery, but desk pass 2 (2026-07-15) tested each candidate lever for outcome evidence (did revenue, margin, volume, or retention actually improve, not just adoption) and narrowed the menu. The full per-lever review with every figure is in `research-readings/2026-07-15-alternative-solutions-evidence.md`; its verdicts are merged here (2026-07-16). No lever below is proven to restore revenue for a boutique winery, so each keeps a live falsifier for July interviews.

- Wine Improvement District (collective demand-building): PARK. A county-wide self-assessment of about 1 to 2% on tasting visits, in-state club memberships, and bottle sales for shared marketing, needing 51% weighted approval (which itself worries small operations) and modeled on the hotel tourism levy running since 2005 (press-democrat-2025; vinepair-2025). It is a region-level lever our single protagonist cannot pull, and it is stalled: still exploratory with no vote as of August 2025, when the Winegrowers paused the consultant's work through harvest after spending $38,450, conceding about 18 months from any approval to full funding (kqed-2025; calagnet-2025). The two sponsoring organizations are now merging into one "Sonoma County Wine" body for 2027 and did not mention the WID (wia-2026). Its flagship proof point, Temecula's claimed near-100% DTC increase, is contested: Sonoma State's Damien Wilson calls the causal link "very, very questionable" (vinepair-2025), and an unbylined op-ed disputes the baseline (treat as unverified allegation) (northbaybiz-2025). Lodi approved a 1.5% tasting-room district in November 2025, but that is adoption, not outcome (lodi-2025). The generic-advertising literature reports 4:1 to 6:1 benefit-to-cost across commodities, but at industry level, not one winery's margin (ward-2006). Keep it on the ecosystem map as context; drop it as a winery strategy.

- Alternative and unconventional varieties: KEEP, conditional on interviews. National demand signals are real: white blends up 13% in dollars and 7.8% in volume year over year, Chenin Blanc purchase frequency up 22%, sparkling above $15 called one of wine's most resilient segments, and Josh Cellars' Seaswept white blend at 195,000 cases in year one (forbes-2025-duecy), alongside the earlier argument that lesser-known grapes "invite discovery rather than comparison" and so escape price benchmarking (forbes-2026). The rationale gained hard support: benchmarked varieties are collapsing, Sonoma Chardonnay near $2,370 a ton on contract against roughly $800 spot, so escaping the comparison has real value (turrentine-2026). One named outcome case, Tablas Creek (a Rhone and Mediterranean specialist selling largely direct), reports 2025 total sales up about 2% against a national DTC channel down 15% by volume, genuine relative outperformance but a single confounded case (tablas-creek-2026; sovos-2026). Still missing anywhere public: sell-through and margin for Vermentino or Nebbiolo specifically. Two Shepherds and Idlewild interviews must test it (Hypothesis 3 falsifier).

- Lean direct-to-consumer: KEEP, but REFRAMED from "go direct" to "out-execute in direct." The naive version is contradicted by the channel: 2025 DTC shipments fell about 967,000 cases, down 15% in volume and 6% in value, the steepest on record, and wineries outside Napa in the "rest of California" segment fell 32% in 2025 (47% since 2021), the profile closest to our protagonist (sovos-2026). Club growth slowed to 2% nationally from 13% in 2021, Napa clubs ran a net loss of 86 members per winery, and reservations fell about 2.1% across 363 wineries with "no sustained recovery" (napa-register-2026). But the same data shows why it survives reframed: DTC still carries about 70% of typical winery revenue, club member lifetime value hit a record $2,803, and the spread is enormous, top-tier wineries growing revenue 22% while bottom-tier fell 13% in the same market; on one testable tactic, 30% of Sonoma respondents cut tasting fees in 2025, and about a quarter of fee-cutters saw visitation clearly improve, another 27% saw it stabilize (napa-register-2026). The channel is shrinking; the story is the winner-loser spread inside it. Interview target: what top-tier operators do differently, and our winery's own club and conversion numbers.

- Low- and no-alcohol: SPLIT. Drop the NA-wine line as a primary pivot: total US NA off-premise is $823 million (up 27.2%), but NA beer holds 84.9% and NA wine only 11.2%, and all NA is 0.7% of alcohol sales, a pond smaller than one large winery's revenue, with the shelf already held by FRE (about half of US NA wine) and Giesen (company-reported) (anba-2025; forbes-2025-duecy; giesen-2025). Keep the format half as a secondary move: low- and no-alcohol is projected at 13.6% CAGR through 2029 versus 2.1% for traditional alcohol (ey-2025-glp1-update), NA beer grew 15% in 2025 while total US beverage alcohol fell 5% (iwsr-2026-may), and premium half-bottles posted double-digit growth in 2025 including Rombauer and The Prisoner (forbes-2025-duecy). Serving-size and value-per-serving formats, tracking ABV and calories as core metrics, match the moderation buyer without leaving wine (ey-parthenon-2025); a standalone NA wine brand does not.

- Price and value repositioning (new lever): ADD. This was not on the original menu and has the strongest quantified support of anything reviewed. Aggregate US wine demand is inelastic (about -0.53 on scanner data; meta-analytic mean -0.65, 83% of estimates inelastic), but demand facing an individual wine product averages about -4.77, so category-wide cuts cannot restore demand while a single winery's repricing can move share powerfully (chandra-2025; fogarty-2008). Buyers point the same way: 51% of reducers agree "I can't afford to spend as much on wine as I have," 47% found cheaper alternatives, and 54% buy under-$15 weekly or monthly against 28% for $30-plus (drinks-business-2026); cost is now the most-cited moderation driver at 31% (iwsr-2026-apr). Tier data says where to sit, the "hourglass": under-$12 deteriorating fastest, $20-to-$29 and $100-plus growing, with Sonoma's average SRP $63.66 versus Napa's $103.15 (svb-2026; napa-register-2026). Caveat: repricing fixes spend-per-buyer and share, not participation (frequency reasons still lean moderation and health), and brand and margin math are unquantified. Test it against Hypothesis 1: did any winery's price move actually shift volume.

- Ride the supply correction (new, an enabler not a demand lever): the industry's actually-executed response is supply-side. The 2025 California crush was the lightest since 1999 at 2.62 million tons, 23% below the five-year average, with about 57,000 acres removed, and spot Sonoma Chardonnay near $800 a ton makes exceptional fruit historically cheap for a lean buyer (turrentine-2026). Timing consensus is a bottom in 2027 through 2028 (svb-2026), with 71% of wineries expecting stabilization or rebound within three years (bmo-2026) and distributor forecasts negative through 2026 (wswa-2026). It creates no demand, but it changes the economics of every other lever and strengthens the lean, low-overhead survival models below. Treat it as the enabling condition for Act III economics.

Those survival models stay in play under these verdicts: micro-launches (Yarrow Wines began with 250 cases on a little over $10,000), no-loan direct selling with the founder's phone number on the bottle (Right Turn Wine Co.), shared custom-crush space, and outside income as the enabling economics, with even an established owner retrenching (Arietta cutting production from 3,200 cases to under 2,000) (sonoma-mag-2025). Which levers our winery pursues, and whether any actually moved its revenue or margin, is still for July interviews.

## Cross-source contradictions to hold (do not resolve prematurely)
These are genuine conflicts across sources. Carry them into interviews as questions, do not paper over them.
1. Severity: crisis versus steady. Sonoma EDC frames it as extreme (US alcohol at a 90-year low) (sonoma-edc-2025); Gallup frames drinking as relatively steady (58% still drink, near the 63% long-run trend; four drinks a week, matching 1996) (gallup-2024-brenan). Different metrics, opposite tone. Update 2026-07-15: partly a metric artifact. Self-reported participation is now falling faster (54%, a record low; 2.8 drinks a week; gallup-2025) than measured volume (-2% to -6% across methods in 2025; svb-2026, iwsr-2026-may). Industry consensus reads it as structural but decelerating, with a bottom expected 2027 through 2028 (svb-2026; wswa-2026; terrain-2026). Neither "steady" nor "collapse" survives.
2. Pace of the youth decline: about 10 points in two years (nearly 60% to 50% drinking, 2023 to 2025) (ey-parthenon-2025) versus about 10 points over two decades (72% to 62%) (gallup-2023-saad). Update 2026-07-15: resolved as a time-window artifact. Gallup's own 2025 poll shows 18-to-34 participation at 50%, down from 59% in 2023 (gallup-2025), matching EY. Two decades of slow drift, then a sharp acceleration after 2023. Both sources were right about their windows.
3. Is wine the steepest-declining category, or holding share? Wine as steepest cutback (52%) (ey-parthenon-2025) and "wine's fall is steeper" (nbc-2025), versus wine's preference share holding at 33%, near its 32% trend (gallup-2024-brenan). Update 2026-07-15: metric-dependent. Wine is steepest on distributor depletions (down 9% in volume for 2025, versus roughly -4% for core spirits; terrain-2026), tied with beer on IWSR consumption (-6% each; iwsr-2026-may), and roughly stable on Gallup preference share (29% wine, 30% liquor, beer first; gallup-2025). No dataset shows wine outperforming the other categories.
4. Magnitude of the US decline: sales down about 6% (SipSource, 2023 to 2024) (nbc-2025) versus apparent consumption down 4.3% (2024 to 2025) (oiv-2025). Different method and period. Update 2026-07-15: explained as a method ladder for the same year (2025): -2.0% on SVB's total-market case estimate (svb-2026), about -3 to -4% on apparent consumption (bw166 via wine-institute-2026; oiv-2025), -6% on IWSR consumption (iwsr-2026-may), -9% on SipSource wholesale depletions, which destocking exaggerates (terrain-2026). Rule for deliverables: never quote a decline number without naming the method.
5. Primary demand driver: price and affordability (forbes-2026, and the affordability signal in oiv-2025) versus health perception (gallup-2024-brenan, nbc-2025). This directly changes the recommended winery response. Update 2026-07-15: the evidence now supports a layered answer rather than either/or. Cost is the most-cited short-run moderation driver (31%; iwsr-2026-apr) while health beliefs keep setting records (53% of all adults, 66% of 18-to-34s; gallup-2025) and SVB puts demographics first, boomers "sunsetting" (svb-2026). For wine buyers specifically: moderation (49%) and health (38%) outrank price (24%) as reasons for drinking less often, but price leads the reasons for spending less per bottle (drinks-business-2026). Which margin matters more for our winery depends on whether its problem is traffic or ticket, an interview question.
6. Stated cause of the youth decline: demographic composition (gallup-2023-saad) versus health perception (gallup-2024-brenan) versus GLP-1 (ey-parthenon-2025). None quantifies the relative weight. Update 2026-07-15: still unresolved on weights. Health beliefs moved furthest and fastest among the measured candidates (66% of 18-to-34s call moderate drinking unhealthy; gallup-2025); cannabis substitution is documented only within user samples (sciencedaily-2026); GLP-1 effects are quantified only among users (ey-2025-glp1-update); no source decomposes the roughly 9-point youth drop.

## Working hypotheses (for Aug 9)
Three falsifiable hypotheses, each cast toward an Act I or Act II question, testable in July interviews.
1. Price, not health, is the binding constraint. For a premium Sonoma winery the operative reason buyers pull back is affordability and price-value, not health or anti-alcohol sentiment. We are wrong if buyer interviews name health, wellness, or a genuine taste shift as the dominant reason they buy less wine, and winery interviews show price cuts or discounting failed to move volume. Supports: (forbes-2026), (oiv-2025), (nbc-2025), (sonoma-edc-2025). Opposes: (gallup-2024-brenan), (ey-parthenon-2025). Added 2026-07-15: new supports: (iwsr-2026-apr) cost as top moderation driver; (drinks-business-2026) affordability battery; (wine-opinions-2025) youth price citations; (chandra-2025) firm-level elasticity near -4.8 makes price a potent single-winery lever. New opposes: (gallup-2025) record health beliefs; (wmc-2025) taste preference blocks millennial adoption; (drinks-business-2026) moderation and health outrank price for drinking less often. Refinement to test: price binds on spend per bottle and share; health and moderation bind on frequency and participation.
2. The threat is the pipeline, not the core. The decline is concentrated in under-35s failing to enter wine, while the winery's older, affluent core stays comparatively stable, so the acute near-term risk is the future-customer pipeline rather than erosion of current buyers. We are wrong if the winery's own club, mailing-list, and DTC data show its established older buyers are themselves cutting back (smaller clubs, lapsed memberships, fewer bottles). Supports: (gallup-2023-saad), (gallup-2024-brenan), (oiv-2025). Added 2026-07-15: new supports: (gallup-2025) under-35 participation at 50%; (wine-opinions-2025). New opposes: (wmc-2025) roughly 9 million fewer wine consumers overall and the boomer share down from 32% to 26%, so the core is eroding too; (napa-register-2026) club net member losses; (sovos-2026) fewer households receiving shipments. Prior opposes: (nbc-2025), (ey-parthenon-2025).
3. Go small, go direct, differentiate. The winery-side strategy that best protects margin is a lean, largely direct-to-consumer model built on small-lot and differentiated (alternative-variety, value-per-serving) offerings, because differentiated direct sales escape price benchmarking and carry better per-bottle economics. We are wrong if winery interviews show DTC, alternative-variety, and small-lot lines carry equal-or-worse margins and sell-through, and buyers show no greater willingness to pay or repurchase for direct or differentiated wine. Supports: (sonoma-mag-2025), (forbes-2026), (ey-parthenon-2025), (sonoma-edc-2025). Added 2026-07-15: new supports: (napa-register-2026) top-tier wineries grew revenue 22% while bottom-tier declined 13% in the same shrinking market, record club lifetime value of $2,803, and measurable visitation response to tasting-fee cuts. New opposes: (sovos-2026) the DTC channel itself fell 15% by volume in 2025 with non-Napa California down 32%, so "direct" is not a rising tide. Prior opposes: (nbc-2025), (press-democrat-2025). Fuller evidence per lever is now in Act III (merged 2026-07-16); the detailed review remains in `research-readings/2026-07-15-alternative-solutions-evidence.md`.

## Open questions and gaps
- Protagonist unset (Monte Rio Cellars vs Quivira), and neither appears in any desk source. Needs a decision plus a founder interview.
- No wine-specific buyer data. EY's purchase-criteria re-ranking is spirits-only and Gallup is all-alcohol, so how wine buyers themselves trade off price, taste, calories, and health is unmeasured (ey-parthenon-2025, gallup-2024-brenan). Update 2026-07-15: partially closed. Wine-specific tradeoff data now exists (drinks-business-2026; wine-opinions-2025; wmc-2025). Still missing: Sonoma-specific and premium-segment buyer data, and our winery's own buyers.
- Price sensitivity is asserted but never quantified: no source estimates demand gained or lost at specific price points. Update 2026-07-15: closed at category and product level (chandra-2025: aggregate about -0.53, individual product about -4.77; fogarty-2008: category mean -0.65). Still missing: any real winery's own price-experiment results, which is now an explicit interview question.
- No outcome evidence that any response works. The Wine Improvement District is only proposed and its effectiveness is called uncertain; alternative varieties, DTC pivots, and NA wine are not shown to restore revenue (press-democrat-2025, sonoma-edc-2025). Update 2026-07-15: the WID was paused in August 2025 with no vote since, and the sponsoring organizations are merging (calagnet-2025; wia-2026). Update 2026-07-16: the full outcome-evidence review per lever is now merged into Act III (park the WID, keep varieties conditionally, reframe lean DTC, split NA, add price and value repositioning and the supply-correction enabler); still no lever is shown to restore winery revenue, so the falsifiers move to interviews.
- The core interpretive conflict is unresolved: is wine demand structurally shrinking, or holding a steady share while the category churns toward other formats? (contradiction 3). Update 2026-07-15: see contradictions 1 and 3; the industry consensus is structural-but-decelerating with a forecast bottom in 2027 through 2028 (svb-2026).
- Tasting-room decline is only county-level (-14%, then -8.4%): no data on who stopped visiting, spend per visit, or wine-club retention (press-democrat-2025). Update 2026-07-15: partially closed at the survey level: reservations down about 2.1% year over year across 363 tracked wineries, club growth at 2% nationally, average tasting-room purchase $197 per visitor (napa-register-2026). Still missing: who stopped visiting and why.
- The relative weight of the competing drivers (health, GLP-1, demographics, price, cannabis) is never isolated (contradiction 6, still open after the 2026-07-15 pass).
- Source caveats to respect in any deliverable: OIV apparent-consumption method (oiv-2025); Forbes carries no decline data, only a preference-shift argument (forbes-2026); Gallup is national all-alcohol, not wine-specific (gallup-2023-saad, gallup-2024-brenan, gallup-2025); SipSource depletions overstate consumer decline during destocking (terrain-2026); Giesen figures are company-reported (see sources.md); the NorthBay biz WID op-ed is unbylined and its Temecula claims are unverified (sources.md).
