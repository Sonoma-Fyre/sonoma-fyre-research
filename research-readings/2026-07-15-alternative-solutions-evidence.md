Method: three parallel desk-research passes on 2026-07-15 (pivot-lever outcome evidence, buyer and price data, contradiction weighing), then direct verification of every load-bearing figure against its source page the same day. Verification flags are noted inline. The question asked of every lever: is there outcome evidence (revenue, margin, volume, retention actually improved), not just adoption stories?

The candidate list going in was the Act III menu: Wine Improvement District, alternative varieties, lean DTC, low- and no-alcohol formats. The evidence supports splitting one, reframing one, parking one, and adding two.

## 1. Wine Improvement District (collective demand-building): PARK

Status is the problem. The Sonoma WID was still exploratory as of August 2025, a 1 to 2 percent consumer surcharge on tasting visits, in-state club memberships, and bottle purchases, with no vote held (kqed-2025-sonoma-wid-mixed-feelings.pdf). On August 19, 2025 the Winegrowers paused the consultant's work through harvest, having spent $38,450 on Civitas at $7,500 a month; their own newsletter concedes that even with agreement it would take "approximately 18 months before the full funding came in" (calagnet-2025-wid-challenges.pdf). We found no vote, adoption, or formal abandonment since (unknown, not dead). The two sponsoring organizations then announced they will unify into a single "Sonoma County Wine" membership starting 2027, and the announcement does not mention the WID at all (wia-2026-sonoma-wine-orgs-unify.pdf).

Outcome evidence is contested. WIDs exist in Temecula and Livermore (2021), Amador, and Santa Barbara, and Lodi's 1.5 percent tasting-room district was approved November 18, 2025 (adoption, not outcome) (vinepair-2025-wine-improvement-districts-debate.pdf; abridged-2025-lodi-wine-district.pdf). Temecula's headline claim of "an almost 100 percent increase in DTC sales" is the sector's main proof point, but it comes from a private estimate spanning the Covid shift, and Sonoma State's Damien Wilson calls the causal link "very, very questionable" and warns a surcharge risks alienating consumers in a category already in decline (vinepair-2025). An unbylined NorthBay biz op-ed further argues Temecula's baseline surveyed only about 19 wineries and its 2024 data leaned on modeled estimates; we could not independently verify those specifics, so treat them as allegation, not fact (northbaybiz-2025-wid-skeptic-oped.pdf). The strongest support is generic: the commodity checkoff literature reports benefit-to-cost ratios of "4:1 to 6:1" across commodities (beef 5.6:1, dairy 4.6:1), with documented free-rider problems (ward-2006-commodity-checkoff-generic-advertising.pdf). That is industry-level surplus, not any single winery's margin, and weighted voting concerns small operations (vinepair-2025).

Verdict: park it. It is a region-level lever our protagonist cannot pull, it is stalled locally, its funding lag runs past the industry's own forecast bottom, and its flagship outcome number does not survive scrutiny. Keep it on the ecosystem map as context; drop it as a winery strategy.

## 2. Alternative and unconventional varieties: KEEP, conditional

Demand signals are real at national scale: white blends up 13 percent in dollars and 7.8 percent in volume year over year (Circana), Chenin Blanc purchase frequency up 22 percent with repeat households up 6 percent (Numerator), organic ultra-premium up "nearly 20%" in dollars, sparkling above $15 called "one of the most resilient segments in all of wine," and Josh Cellars' Seaswept white blend hit 195,000 cases in year one (forbes-2025-where-wine-won.pdf).

One named outcome case: Tablas Creek, a Rhone and Mediterranean variety specialist selling largely direct, reports 2025 total sales up about 2 percent (wholesale up 15, DTC down 1, tasting traffic down 3.3) in a year when the national DTC channel fell 15 percent by volume (tablas-creek-2026-tariffs-and-2025-sales.pdf; sovos-2026-dtc-wine-shipping-report.pdf). Roughly flat against a falling tide is genuine relative outperformance, but it is one Paso Robles winery whose brand, model, and variety choice are confounded.

The rationale also gained support: benchmarked varieties are collapsing in price, with Sonoma County Chardonnay averaging $2,370 a ton on contract against a spot market "closer to $800 per ton" (turrentine-2026-crush-report-2025.pdf). Escaping comparison has measurable value when the comparison is that ugly. What is still missing anywhere public: sell-through or margin data for Vermentino or Nebbiolo specifically.

Verdict: keep, conditional on interview validation. The July winery interviews (Two Shepherds and Idlewild are exactly the right contrast cases) must test whether alternative-variety lots actually carry better margins and sell-through, per Hypothesis 3's falsifier.

## 3. Lean direct-to-consumer: KEEP, REFRAMED

The naive version ("go direct and grow") is contradicted by the channel data. 2025 DtC shipments fell 967,000 cases, down 15 percent in volume and $230 million (6 percent) in value, the steepest contraction on record; wineries outside Napa in the "rest of California" segment fell 32 percent in 2025 and 47 percent since 2021, the profile closest to our protagonist (sovos-2026-dtc-wine-shipping-report.pdf). Club membership growth has slowed to 2 percent nationally from 13 percent in 2021, Napa clubs ran a net loss of 86 members per winery, and tasting reservations fell about 2.1 percent across 363 tracked wineries with "no sustained recovery" (napa-register-2026-svb-june-dtc-survey.pdf).

But the same dataset shows why the lever survives in reframed form. DTC still carries about 70 percent of typical winery revenue; club member lifetime value hit a record $2,803; and the performance spread is enormous, with top-tier wineries growing revenue 22 percent while bottom-tier wineries declined 13 percent in the same market (napa-register-2026). There is even experiment-like evidence on one tactic: 30 percent of Sonoma respondents cut tasting fees in 2025, and among fee reducers about a quarter saw visitation clearly improve and another 27 percent saw it stabilize (napa-register-2026). McMillan's framing fits: "This is not a cycle you can wait out" (svb-2026-state-of-us-wine-industry.pdf).

Verdict: keep, reframed from "go direct" to "out-execute in direct." The channel is shrinking; the story is the winner-loser spread inside it. Interview target: what the top-tier operators do differently, and our winery's own club and conversion numbers.

## 4. Low- and no-alcohol wine and formats: SPLIT

Kill the primary version. NA wine is growing fast from a tiny base: total US NA off-premise is $823 million (up 27.2 percent), of which NA beer holds 84.9 percent and NA wine 11.2 percent, and the whole NA segment is 0.7 percent of alcohol sales (anba-2025-na-industry-statistics.pdf). The shelf is already concentrated: FRE "holds roughly half of the U.S. non-alcoholic wine market" and Giesen 0% claims 49 percent of the premium NA still segment after five years and nine styles (forbes-2025-where-wine-won.pdf; giesen-2025-na-wine-five-years.pdf, company-reported). A boutique entrant faces dealcoholization tolling costs against entrenched leaders in a market smaller than a single large winery's revenue. The team's existing caution (nbc-2025) stands on base size, though the growth-rate half has softened: NA wine now grows at a rate comparable to NA beer.

Keep the format half as a secondary move. The category tailwind is real (low and no alcohol projected at 13.6 percent CAGR 2024 to 2029 versus 2.1 percent for traditional alcohol; NA beer grew 15 percent in 2025 while total US beverage alcohol fell 5 percent) (ey-2025-glp1-alcohol-update.pdf; iwsr-2026-us-volume-down-5-percent.pdf), and the format evidence sits closer to home: premium half-bottles posted double-digit growth in 2025, including Rombauer and The Prisoner (forbes-2025-where-wine-won.pdf). Serving-size innovation matches the moderation buyer without leaving wine.

Verdict: split. Drop "launch an NA wine line" as a primary pivot; keep half-bottles, single-serve, and value-per-serving formats as a supporting move.

## 5. NEW: Price and value repositioning (add to the list)

This lever was not on the menu and has the strongest quantified support of anything reviewed. The economics: aggregate US wine demand is inelastic, about -0.53 on scanner data, and the meta-analytic range agrees (mean -0.65, median -0.55, 83 percent of estimates inelastic), but demand facing an individual wine product averages about -4.77 (chandra-2025-us-wine-demand-elasticity.pdf; fogarty-2008-beer-wine-spirits-meta-analysis.pdf). Price cuts cannot save the category; they can powerfully move share for one winery. This is the number the gaps list said did not exist.

The buyer evidence points the same way. Among wine drinkers spending less, 51 percent agree "I can't afford to spend as much on wine as I have in the past" and 47 percent found cheaper alternatives they like; 54 percent of all respondents buy under-$15 wine weekly or monthly against 28 percent for $30-plus (drinks-business-2026-wine-opinions-value-survey.pdf). Among 21-to-39 reducers, 47 percent cite rising prices of the wines they like, and wine loses the value-perception contest to beer, 50 versus 57 percent rating it high value (wine-opinions-2025-under-40-threats.pdf). Cost is now the most-cited moderation driver among US drinkers at 31 percent (iwsr-2026-economic-pressures-moderation.pdf). Tier data says where to sit: under-$12 is deteriorating fastest while $20 to $29 and $100-plus show growth expectations, the "hourglass" (svb-2026-state-of-us-wine-industry.pdf); the fastest-growing brands are priced well above the $8.64 table-wine retail average (shanken-2026-wine-declines-moderate.pdf); super-premium volume rose 1 percent against the negative trend (iwsr-2026-economic-pressures-moderation.pdf). Sonoma's average SRP in the SVB dataset is $63.66 versus Napa's $103.15 (napa-register-2026-svb-june-dtc-survey.pdf); where our protagonist prices against the hourglass is an interview question, not a desk answer.

Caveats: frequency reasons still lean moderation and health over price (49 versus 38 versus 24 percent; drinks-business-2026), so repricing fixes spend-per-buyer and share, not participation. Brand damage and margin math from repositioning are unquantified anywhere. Verdict: add as a first-class alternative and test it against Hypothesis 1 in interviews, especially whether any winery's price moves actually moved volume.

## 6. NEW: Ride the supply correction (enabler, not a demand lever)

The industry's actually-executed response so far is supply-side. The 2025 California crush was "the lightest on record since 1999" at 2.62 million tons, 8 percent below 2024 and 23 percent below the five-year average, with an estimated 57,000 acres removed and crop value down 22 percent against the five-year average (turrentine-2026-crush-report-2025.pdf). Spot Sonoma Chardonnay near $800 a ton makes exceptional fruit historically cheap for a lean buyer. Timing consensus: SVB expects "the market bottoming in 2027 through 2028" (svb-2026), 71 percent of surveyed wineries expect stabilization or rebound within three years (bmo-2026-wine-market-report.pdf), and distributor forecasts stay negative through 2026 (wswa-2026-sipsource-forecast.pdf). This does not create demand; it changes the economics of every other lever and strengthens the sonoma-mag-2025 micro-launch cases. Treat it as the enabling condition in Act III's economics.

## What desk research still cannot answer (carry to interviews)

Our winery's actual buyer mix and price points against the hourglass; whether any price move they tried shifted volume (H1 falsifier); club, mailing-list, and DTC trends for their older core (H2 falsifier); margins and sell-through on alternative-variety and small-lot wines (H3 falsifier); what top-tier DTC operators do differently in hospitality and conversion; whether any Sonoma winery credits a collective marketing effort with measurable sales.
